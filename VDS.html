Discovering Design
Like many organizations
We were growing faster than we could keep up. Projects grew larger in scope, and while protocols were thorough, they had many different versions and undocumented alterations.

They lacked organization, and rather than producing a standard of competence amongst students, their lack of approachability led to an overreliance on mentors and older, independent researchers.

Highly productive individuals were performing in unorganized environments, and competent people, whose ambition was obvious, were falling victim to cycles of clutter.

Waking up
Competent research was decreasing while knowledge grew more concentrated at the top. It wasn't just a knowledge or ability gap causing people to fall behind. There was something deeper.

Frustration, amongst administrators was increasing as once productive individuals grew apathetic faster than in the past.

Students were no longer learning, and everybody felt it. Something needed to be done. And with resources spread thin, a restructure was only ever going to come from within.
Design Principle 1
Know when you need crowd intelligence.
When you redraw, rethink, and redo
Organizations are revived when passionate people inspire passion in others.

While I was ambitious, there was no feasible way to turn our research stream around alone. While the project began long before there was a dedicated redesign team, no meaningful strides were made until conversations out loud started to emerge.

Think you know the problem?
Try again. Entropy is a funny thing, and organizations degrade in funny ways. Sometimes full attention is put into protocols when leadership relations is where you should focus.

When rethinking, rely on the intelligence and experience of others. Look up and down stream. Often issues cascade from other areas rarely explored.

Design Principle 2
What's obvious for you isn't obvious to everyone
Document everything you do
Early on, our group of ten mentors-turned-designers figured out fast that common sense wasn't all that common.

The greatest strength of a design process is its variety in competence and experience. Everyone has a thing they're better at and vice versa. Committees are great for productivity, but not if they reduce diversity.
